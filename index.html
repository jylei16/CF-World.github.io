Are Text-to-Image Models Inductivist Turkeys?
A Counterfactual Benchmark for Causal Reasoning

Overview: The Inductivist Turkey Dilemma

The philosopher Bertrand Russell once proposed the famous thought experiment of the Inductivist Turkey: Observing daily feedings, the turkey deduces an unbreakable law—that food will always arrive—until Thanksgiving exposes its "world knowledge" as a mere statistical correlation rather than a true understanding of the farmer's intent.

Current text-to-image (T2I) models face a remarkably similar dilemma. While they consistently generate high-quality images that comply with human commonsense—often leading to claims that AI has mastered a comprehensive world model—their underlying mechanisms remain questionable. Does this seemingly perfect understanding stem from a genuine grasp of objective physical laws and causal logic? Or is it merely sophisticated pattern matching and mechanical memorization of high-frequency co-occurrences in their massive training datasets? As recent evaluations in counterfactual scenarios reveal, these models suffer from severe "concept lock-in." Because they primarily learn pixel-level correlations, they struggle to decouple independent causal variables from basic visual attributes. When tasked with generating worlds that systematically contradict real-world priors, they, much like the turkey on Thanksgiving morning, default to familiar statistical habits rather than demonstrating true logical deduction.

The CF-World Benchmark: A Progressive Framework for Probing Genuine Causal Reasoning

To rigorously determine whether text-to-image models truly comprehend the world or merely regurgitate memorized priors, we introduce the Counterfactual-World (CF-World) benchmark. Rather than testing shallow semantic combinations, CF-World systematically evaluates underlying causal logic across a massive, multi-disciplinary dataset, utilizing a strict control variable methodology.

Scale and Disciplinary Diversity

At its core, CF-World is built upon 1,091 fundamental scientific principles, which are systematically expanded into 3,273 meticulously crafted prompts. To ensure a comprehensive evaluation of objective world knowledge, the dataset spans eight distinct disciplines. The Three-Level Progressive Prompting Design

The defining philosophy of CF-World is its progressive, three-tiered evaluation structure. By systematically altering the rules and removing explicit instructions, this framework isolates different cognitive capabilities:

  • Factual (L1) — The Baseline: Requests a standard, real-world phenomenon (e.g., "Draw a piece of wood in water"). This verifies whether the model possesses the foundational knowledge and basic generative capability to render the subject under normal conditions.
  • Explicit Counterfactual (L2) — Visual Decoupling: Introduces an altered physical law and explicitly dictates the resulting visual state (e.g., "If wood were denser than water, draw a piece of wood in water. It should sink to the bottom."). This tests the model's ability to overcome entrenched commonsense priors and execute a counter-intuitive visual combination.
  • Implicit Counterfactual (L3) — Causal Deduction: Presents only the altered premise without providing the visual answer (e.g., "If wood were denser than water, draw a piece of wood in water."). Here, the model must autonomously deduce the causal chain and render the correct counterfactual state without any visual hand-holding.

CF-Eval: A VLM-Based Automated Evaluation Pipeline for Counterfactual Reasoning

To comprehensively quantify the generative capabilities of models in both factual and counterfactual scenarios, we introduce CF-Eval, a robust automated evaluation pipeline driven by Vision-Language Models (VLMs). The pipeline evaluates generated images through a structured, three-step process:

1. Multi-Dimensional Evaluation

The VLM assesses each generated image across three weighted dimensions to ensure a holistic evaluation:

  • Visual Integrity: Evaluates fundamental, style-agnostic technical image quality (e.g., sharpness, anatomy) as a basic viability threshold.
  • Assessment Point: The primary ground truth. It strictly verifies whether the main subject adheres to the specific factual or counterfactual rule requested by the prompt.
  • Logic Consistency: Verifies that the surrounding context and environment align globally with the established physical or counterfactual setting.

2. Score Calculation

The base score for a single image is calculated via a weighted average of the three dimensions. To prevent false positives and ensure meaningful counterfactual assessment, CF-Eval employs a conditional thresholding mechanism. Counterfactual scores (L2 and L3) are only calculated if the model first demonstrates basic factual comprehension by meeting a baseline threshold on the factual prompt (L1 score ≥ 0.5). Otherwise, the counterfactual scores are strictly set to zero.

3. Evaluation Metrics

To rigorously quantify a model's true reasoning capabilities without falling into the "low-base performance trap," we propose two novel metrics based on the geometric mean of absolute scores and relative ratios:

  • Prior Resistance Rate (PRR): Measures a model's ability to overcome entrenched real-world priors when given explicit counterfactual instructions. It isolates the performance shift from standard factual generation (L1) to explicit counterfactual generation (L2).
  • Reasoning Retention Rate (RRR): Assesses the robustness of a model's causal reasoning by measuring how effectively it maintains counterfactual generation capabilities when explicit visual cues are removed (transitioning from L2 to L3).

Key Results

Extensive evaluations across 13 state-of-the-art models reveal a stark reality: while models excel at generating factual scenes, they experience a precipitous performance collapse when confronted with counterfactuals. Based on the quantitative results, we identified several critical insights:

  • The Stubbornness of Prior Dependence: There is a universal performance drop from factual (L1) to explicit counterfactual (L2) generation. Most open-source models yield PRR scores below 0.50, indicating that visual representations and real-world knowledge remain deeply entangled.
  • The "Stronger Prior" Trap: Models with superior foundational capabilities (e.g., high L1 scores) do not necessarily excel in counterfactual tasks. Deep overfitting to training data can actually worsen "concept lock-in," acting as a severe bottleneck for generating counterfactuals.
  • Absence of Implicit Causal Reasoning: Performance drops sharply again in the implicit counterfactual (L3) setting. Without explicit visual cues, current models struggle with autonomous causal deduction, leading to substantial degradation in the Reasoning Retention Rate (RRR).
  • Architectural Implications: Traditional diffusion models with massively scaled text encoders (e.g., FLUX.2-dev) currently outperform native unified multimodal architectures (e.g., OmniGen2, Show-o2) in mitigating attribute entanglement during stress tests.
  • The Open vs. Closed-Source Moat: Top-tier proprietary models (such as Nano Banana Pro and GPT-Image-1.5) maintain significantly more robust scores in both L2 and L3 compared to their open-source counterparts, likely due to massive-scale, high-quality alignment data.
